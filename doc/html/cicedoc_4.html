Horizontal transport

The horizontal transport model consists of 46 continuity equations for various ice and snow quantities such as area, volume, internal energy, and surface temperature. For the transport component equations and additional details, see the PostScript documentation.

Two transport schemes are available, MPDATA [20] and the incremental remapping scheme of [11] (henceforth DB) as modified for sea ice by [12]. Because we now advect so many fields, time spent using MPDATA makes up the largest fraction of computer time for a run. Although a first-order upwind scheme is available as an MPDATA option (see Table 2), we recommend using the incremental remapping method instead. This scheme has several desirable features:

  • It conserves the quantity being transported (area, volume, or energy).
  • It is non-oscillatory; that is, it does not create spurious ripples in the transported fields.
  • It preserves tracer monotonicity. That is, it does not create new extrema in the thickness and enthalpy fields; the values at time m+1 are bounded by the values at time m.
  • It is second-order accurate in space and therefore is much less diffusive than first-order schemes. (The accuracy may be reduced locally to first order to preserve monotonicity.)
  • It is efficient for large numbers of categories or tracers. Much of the work is geometrical and is performed only once per grid cell instead of being repeated for each quantity being transported.
The time step is limited by the requirement that trajectories projected backward from grid cell corners are confined to the four surrounding cells. (This is what is meant by incremental remapping as opposed to general remapping.) This requirement leads to a CFL-like condition. For highly divergent velocity fields the maximum time step is reduced by a factor of two to ensure that trajectories do not cross. However, ice velocity fields in climate models usually have small divergences per time step relative to the grid size.

The remapping scheme described by DB must be modified for sea ice transport. For example, the original scheme has no analogue to the energy conservation equations. Energy is proportional to the product of two tracers, thickness and enthalpy, and DB did not consider tracer products. Also, the original scheme was designed for slowly varying curvilinear grids rather than the CICE grid, which is highly curved near the poles. But with the appropriate changes, incremental remapping gives excellent results, as described in [12].

The remapping algorithm can be summarized as follows:

  1. Given ice velocities at grid cell corners, identify departure regions for the fluxes across each cell face. Divide these departure regions into triangles and compute the coordinates of the triangle vertices.
  2. Given values of the ice area and tracers at the cell centers, construct linear approximations of the area and tracer fields in each grid cell. Limit the field gradients to preserve monotonicity.
  3. Integrate these fields over the departure triangles to obtain the area, volume, and energy fluxes across each cell face.
  4. Transfer the fluxes across cell faces and update the state variables.
Step (1) is done once per time step, whereas steps (2)-(4) are repeated for each thickness category.

Introduction

The Los Alamos sea ice model (CICE) is the result of an effort to develop a computationally efficient sea ice component for a fully coupled atmosphere-ice-ocean-land global climate model. It was designed to be compatible with the Parallel Ocean Program (POP), an ocean circulation model developed at Los Alamos National Laboratory for use on massively parallel computers . The current version of the model has been enhanced greatly through collaborations with members of the Community Climate System Model (CCSM) Polar Climate Working Group, based at the National Center for Atmospheric Research (NCAR).

CICE has several interacting components: a thermodynamic model that computes local growth rates of snow and ice due to vertical conductive fluxes, snowfall and local temperatures; a model of ice dynamics, which predicts the velocity field of the ice pack based on a model of the material strength of the ice; a transport model that describes advection of the areal concentration, ice volumes and other state variables; and a ridging parameterization that transfers ice among thickness categories based on energetic balances and rates of strain. Additional routines prepare and execute data exchanges with an external "flux coupler," which then passes the data to other climate model components such as POP.

The Coupling section discusses the sea ice model's role in the global coupled model system, including the atmosphere-ice boundary layer. The Model Components section describes each of the major physical components of the sea ice model: transport (both horizontally and in thickness space), dynamics, ridging and thermodynamics. The Numerical Implementation section discusses the directory structure, grid, initialization and coupling, numerical parameters, model output and execution procedures, and The Troubleshooting section contains some pointers for debugging.

Generally speaking, subroutine names are given in italic and file names are boldface in this document. Symbols used in the code are typewritten, while corresponding symbols in the PostScript version of this document are in the math font which, granted, looks a lot like italic. A glossary of symbols is provided, along with many of their values. The organization of this software distribution is described in the Directory Structure section; most files and subroutines referred to in this documentation are part of the CICE code found in cice/source/, unless otherwise noted.

For those who wonder what "CICE" stands for, it is not an acronym... a little rebellion on my part here at this totally acronym-oriented government lab. It is shorthand for "sea ice," an amused nod to people outside the field who when told that I study sea ice, think I'm saying "C" ice and have no idea what the letter C has to do with ice. There is a small grass-roots movement underway to alter the model name's pronunciation from "sea ice" to what an Italian might say, "chee chay." Arguments on either side of this issue are welcome.
